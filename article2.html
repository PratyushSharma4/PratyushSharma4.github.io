Logic and paradox

When will the prisoner die?

Core insight

The prisoner's reasoning tries to use the judge's prediction to discredit the very possibility that the prediction could come true. Because he convinces himself it is impossible, he loses his ability to predict it, which paradoxically allows the surprise to happen.

Question

A judge tells a condemned prisoner that he will be executed at noon on one weekday of the following week, and that the execution will be a surprise. The prisoner reasons that it cannot happen on Friday, because if he survives until Thursday night then Friday would be forced and therefore unsurprising. Eliminating Friday, he then eliminates Thursday, then Wednesday, and so on, concluding that the execution cannot happen at all. Yet when the executioner arrives on Wednesday, he is genuinely surprised. Where did the logic break?

The Logical Fallacy

The prisoner treats the word "surprise" like a fixed fact, such as a number or a date. In reality, a surprise simply means a lack of knowledge. The prisoner reasons: "If I know \( X \), then \( X \) is not a surprise." By following his logic backward, he reaches the state: "I know that no day is possible." Because he convinces himself that an execution is impossible on any day, he no longer knows what will happen. And because he loses that knowledge, the execution can be a surprise again.

The prisoner's proof requires him to fully trust the judge at every step. But his final result, that he won't be hanged at all, directly contradicts the judge. A logical argument falls apart if its conclusion proves that its starting assumption must be false. The moment he convinces himself he is perfectly safe, he loses his ability to predict the execution, which allows the judge's "surprise" to come true.

The Liar's Paradox connection

This puzzle is a variation of the Liar's Paradox ("This sentence is false"). To eliminate Friday, the prisoner assumes the judge is telling the complete truth. But if he continues eliminating every day using this method, he concludes the judge is lying, because he thinks no execution can happen.

However, if the judge is lying, the prisoner was never allowed to use the judge's original statement to eliminate Friday in the first place! You cannot use a rule to prove that the rule itself is a lie.
