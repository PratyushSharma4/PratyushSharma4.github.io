Probability and expectation

The flaw in the casino game

Core insight

The issue is not that fair games suddenly become unfair. The issue is that an infinite betting process does not let you use expectation as casually as a finite one.

Question

Suppose a casino game gives a 50% chance of winning and a 50% chance of losing on each round. You start by betting $1, and every time you lose you double the next bet. Since the first win recovers all previous losses and adds $1 of profit, it is tempting to conclude that unlimited capital turns the strategy into a guaranteed gain. If each individual round has expectation zero, where exactly does the reasoning fail?

The mathematical flaw

The expected value of any finite number of rounds in a fair game is exactly 0. The martingale strategy claims an expected profit of $1 using a stopping time \( \tau \) (the moment you finally win). To calculate the expected value of a stopped martingale using the formula \( E[X_\tau] = E[X_0] = 0 \), Doob's Optional Stopping Theorem requires at least one of these three conditions to hold:

  • The stopping time \( \tau \) must be bounded by a fixed maximum number of steps.
  • The expected stopping time \( E[\tau] \) must be finite, and the absolute differences between consecutive steps must be bounded by a constant.
  • The sequence of values must be bounded absolutely (the process cannot drop below or rise above fixed limits).

The doubling strategy violates all three conditions. First, you could lose indefinitely, meaning there is no maximum number of bets. Second, a symmetric one-dimensional random walk takes infinitely long on average to reach a target state, meaning \( E[\tau] = \infty \), and your bet sizes grow exponentially without any upper bound. Third, without a table limit or a finite bankroll, your potential debt plunges to negative infinity before recovering.

Because it fails the Optional Stopping Theorem, you cannot simply say that the strategy's expected outcome matches a finite game. The naive intuition fails because it ignores the indeterminate \( \infty - \infty \) math created by the extreme tail risk of losing repeatedly.
