Probability and paradox

The flaw in the two envelopes paradox

The usual switching argument sounds almost too clean to resist. You open one envelope, call the amount \( x \), and a short expected-value calculation seems to prove that switching is always better. The paradox disappears the moment you force the symbols to keep the same meaning all the way through.

Core insight

The symbol \( x \) is treated as if it were fixed, even though the argument makes it stand for two different envelope values in two different scenarios.

The question

Imagine you are presented with two identical envelopes. One contains some amount of money and the other contains exactly twice that amount. You choose one at random, open it, and find an amount we call \( x \). You are then offered the chance to keep what you have or switch to the unopened envelope.

A tempting calculation says switching must be the superior strategy. With probability one half, the other envelope contains \( 2x \). With probability one half, it contains \( 0.5x \). So the expected value of switching appears to be

\[ E = 0.5(2x) + 0.5(0.5x) = 1.25x \]

If that were valid, switching would always increase your expected winnings. But that cannot be right. If the reasoning worked, it would also tell you to switch back again immediately.

Why the argument feels convincing

The expression looks innocent because each case is individually plausible. If your envelope happens to hold the smaller amount, then the other really does contain double. If your envelope happens to hold the larger amount, then the other really does contain half. The hidden problem is that the same variable is being forced to serve both stories at once.

In the \( 2x \) term, the argument is imagining that your current envelope contains the smaller amount. In the \( 0.5x \) term, it is imagining that your current envelope contains the larger amount. Those are different worlds, and the value represented by \( x \) is not the same in both.

The clean way to write it

Let the smaller envelope contain \( A \), so the larger one contains \( 2A \). Now there are two genuinely distinct possibilities when you pick an envelope.

With probability one half, you chose the smaller envelope. Then your current amount is \( A \) and the other envelope contains \( 2A \).

With probability one half, you chose the larger envelope. Then your current amount is \( 2A \) and the other envelope contains \( A \).

The expected value of your current envelope is therefore

\[ E_{\text{current}} = 0.5(A) + 0.5(2A) = 1.5A \]

The expected value of the other envelope is

\[ E_{\text{other}} = 0.5(2A) + 0.5(A) = 1.5A \]

They are identical. There is no mathematical advantage to switching.

Where the logic fails

The flawed formula

\[ E = 0.5(2x) + 0.5(0.5x) \]

quietly changes the meaning of \( x \) halfway through. In the first term, \( x \) behaves like \( A \). In the second term, \( x \) behaves like \( 2A \). Once that shift is exposed, the paradox loses its force. The algebra was never combining like quantities in the first place.

Takeaway

The two envelopes paradox does not show that expectation is unreliable. It shows that expected value only helps when the random variables are defined consistently. Here, the seductive step is using one symbol for two different values and then pretending nothing changed.
